The heliocentric model triggered a revolution, the Copernican Revolution, that led to more work and many more discoveries in the field of astronomy over the next one century. Johannes Kepler (1571 - 1630), a German scientist, concluded that planets moved in elliptical orbits and not in circular or epicyclic ones. Kepler's Three Laws of Planetary Motion endorsed the physical reality of the Copernican model and corroborated it with real astronomical data.

Galileo Galilei (1564 - 1642), the father of modern observational astronomy and the father of science, discovered the Phases of Venus through his telescope and documented Jupiter's Galilean Moons for the first time. His observations supported the Copernican cosmology over its predecessors.

The Copernican Revolution came to an end only when Sir Isaac Newton (1642 - 1726) formulated the Law of Universal Gravitation and his famous Three Laws of Motion. Gravitation removed the last doubts about the validity of the heliocentric model of Solar System.
