Polarity Reversals

Since we recently looked at the tennis racket theorem for rotating bodies, it's a good time to learn about another kind of flip that happen on Earth. The Earth's magnetic field, powered by the rotating molten iron core at its center, also known as geodynamo, has flipped polarity many a times over in the past. Meaning its magnetic north flipped over to the place where magnetic south currently is and vice-versa. This is also called Geomagnetic Reversal.

Studies of artifacts and rocks on Earth show that polarity reversals have happened every few hundred thousand years and the last one occurred ~786,000 years ago. Scientists believe that the next geomagnetic reversal is long due and it should happen soon. Observations suggest that Earth's magnetic field has weakened by over 5% in the last one century. It is thought that during a polarity reversal Earth's magnetic field weakens by over 90% before reappearing on the other side strong again.

According to NASA during a polarity reversal there isn't a likelihood that the magnetic field would disappear completely.
