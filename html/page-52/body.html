Jupiter has a total of 67 Moons (as on 2014) orbiting it. The four largest moons of Jupiter are called the Galilean moons or Jovian satellites. They were first discovered by Galileo Galilei back in 1610 - an evidence in favor of the Copernican Heliocentric model. These moons are named Ganymede, Callisto, Io and Europa. The Ganymede, for a moon, is larger than the planet Mercury with a diameter of 3,273 miles. It is still called a moon because it is orbiting a planet and not directly the Sun.

Jupiter takes 11.86 years to go around the Sun. On the other hand it takes only 9.93 hours to spin about its axis. A Jovian day is almost three times as fast as Earth! High speed of rotation and a gaseeous-fluid make up causes Jupiter to bulge at the equator into an oblate-spheroid shape. Jupiter also has a strong magnetic field (14 times as strong as on Earth), and it exhibits Aurorae at its poles.

Given that Jupiter is almost 300 times as massive as Earth it has a very strong gravity.
